Chernobyl Nuclear Disaster

Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant with the New Safe Confinement structure

The Chernobyl disaster was a nuclear accident that occurred on April 26, 1986, at the No. 4 reactor in the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant, near the city of Pripyat in the north of the Ukrainian SSR in the Soviet Union.

It is considered the worst nuclear disaster in history both in terms of cost and casualties. It is one of only two nuclear energy accidents rated at seven—the maximum severity—on the International Nuclear Event Scale, the other being the 2011 Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster in Japan.

Key Facts

  • Date: April 26, 1986
  • Location: Pripyat, Ukrainian SSR, Soviet Union
  • Type: Nuclear and radiation accident
  • Cause: Flawed reactor design, inadequately trained personnel
  • Result: Explosion and fire releasing radioactive contamination
  • Deaths: 31 direct deaths, thousands of indirect deaths from radiation exposure

Timeline of Events

April 25, 1986

Preparation for Test

Reactor power was reduced in preparation for a safety test, which was intended to test the ability to keep the reactor cool in case of a power outage.

April 26, 1986, 1:23 AM

Explosion

During the test, an unexpected power surge occurred, and emergency shutdown was attempted. A more extreme spike in power output followed, leading to a series of explosions in the core.

April 26-27, 1986

Initial Response

Firefighters arrived to put out the fires, many unaware of the radiation danger. Many would later die from acute radiation syndrome.

April 27, 1986

Evacuation Begins

Evacuation of Pripyat begins, with residents told to bring only essential items for a temporary evacuation.

May-December 1986

Containment Efforts

Construction of the concrete sarcophagus to contain the radioactive remains of Reactor 4 is completed in December.

2016

New Safe Confinement

The New Safe Confinement (NSC) structure is moved into place over the old sarcophagus to prevent further radiation leaks.

Economic Impact

The Soviet Union spent an estimated 18 billion rubles on containment and decontamination, crippling the already weakened Soviet economy. Belarus has estimated the total economic cost at $235 billion.
